Efficiently Reconstructing Dynamic Scenes One D4RT at a Time

Alexander Wentzell1,  Tobias Klamt1,  Laura Oledzki1,  Daniel Cremers1,2,  Rudolph Triebel1,2

1 Technical University of Munich, Department of Informatics
2 Munich Data Science Institute (MDSI)

Abstract

We present D4RT (Dynamic 4D Reconstruction and Tracking), a novel approach for reconstructing dynamic 3D scenes from monocular video sequences. Unlike prior methods that rely on dense per-frame optimization or require multi-view camera rigs, D4RT introduces an efficient query-based mechanism that decouples scene geometry estimation from object-level tracking. By leveraging a compact encoder-decoder architecture with temporal attention, our method achieves real-time inference while maintaining state-of-the-art accuracy in both depth estimation and object tracking. Experiments on standard benchmarks (ScanNet, KITTI, and Dynamic Replica) demonstrate that D4RT reduces inference time by 3.2x compared to DenseSLAM while improving depth accuracy by 12% and tracking precision by 8%.

1. Introduction

Reconstructing dynamic 3D scenes from video remains one of the most challenging problems in computer vision. While static scene reconstruction has seen remarkable progress through Neural Radiance Fields (NeRF) and Gaussian Splatting, extending these methods to dynamic environments introduces fundamental difficulties: moving objects violate the multi-view consistency assumptions that underpin most reconstruction pipelines, and per-frame optimization becomes prohibitively expensive as scene complexity grows.

Existing approaches typically fall into two categories: scene-level methods that attempt to model the entire 4D volume with deformable fields, and object-centric methods that track individual entities and reconstruct them independently. Scene-level methods such as D-NeRF and Nerfies achieve impressive visual fidelity but require minutes of optimization per frame, making them impractical for real-time applications. Object-centric methods like DAMP and D3DP offer better scalability but suffer from compounding tracking errors that degrade reconstruction quality over time.

The key insight of D4RT is that reconstruction and tracking need not be treated as a single coupled optimization. By separating the two into parallel pathways with a shared latent representation, we achieve both computational efficiency and mutual regularization.

In this paper, we propose D4RT, a framework that unifies dynamic reconstruction and multi-object tracking through a query-based architecture. Our method processes each video frame through a shared encoder that produces a compact latent representation, which is then consumed by two decoupled decoder heads: one for dense depth estimation and another for object-level tracking. A set of learned queries mediates the information exchange between these heads, enabling the depth branch to benefit from tracking-aware geometry priors and the tracking branch to exploit dense spatial features.

2. Method Overview

D4RT consists of three core components: (1) a spatiotemporal encoder that processes video frames and produces a shared latent representation, (2) a query mechanism that routes information between task-specific heads, and (3) parallel decoder heads for depth reconstruction and object tracking respectively. The entire pipeline is trained end-to-end with a multi-task loss function.

2.1 Query Mechanism

Central to D4RT's architecture is the query mechanism, inspired by the object queries in DETR and its successors. We maintain a fixed set of K learnable queries Q = {q_1, q_2, ..., q_K} that serve as attention-based slots. Each query attends to the encoder output through multi-head cross-attention, and is updated iteratively over L refinement layers. The query outputs are then split and projected into task-specific feature spaces:

  • Depth queries extract geometry features that are decoded into a dense depth map via a lightweight convolutional decoder.
  • Track queries are decoded into object bounding boxes and identity embeddings, enabling persistent tracking across frames.
  • Shared queries (a subset of Q) participate in both decoding paths, providing a bridge for cross-task information flow.

2.2 Architecture

The encoder is built upon a modified Vision Transformer (ViT) backbone with temporal convolution layers. Given a sequence of T consecutive frames, we first extract per-frame patch embeddings using a ViT encoder pretrained on ImageNet-22k. These frame-level features are then aggregated through a temporal convolution module with a kernel size of 3, producing a spatiotemporal feature volume of shape T × H' × W' × C.

The decoder architecture follows an asymmetric design: the depth decoder uses a convolutional upsampling path (similar to FPN) to recover per-pixel depth predictions, while the tracking decoder employs a transformer-based set prediction head that directly regresses bounding boxes and track identities. Both decoders share the same query features, ensuring that geometric context informs tracking predictions and vice versa.

3. Key Results

We evaluate D4RT on three benchmarks and compare against representative baselines spanning both reconstruction-only and tracking-only methods, as well as recent unified approaches. All models are evaluated with identical hardware (NVIDIA A100 GPU, 80 GB). The following table summarizes the main results on the Dynamic Replica benchmark:

Method Depth Acc. (Abs. Rel.) Track Acc. (MOTA%) Speed (fps) Params (M)
D-NeRF (Nerfies) 0.142 -- 0.03 8.7
DenseSLAM 0.118 72.4 5.2 14.3
D3DP 0.109 78.1 12.8 22.1
D4RT (Ours) 0.096 84.3 16.6 18.7

D4RT achieves the best depth accuracy (0.096 absolute relative error) and the highest tracking accuracy (84.3% MOTA), while running at 16.6 fps -- more than 3x faster than DenseSLAM. The parameter count of 18.7M sits between DenseSLAM and D3DP, reflecting the efficiency of the shared encoder design.

4. Code Example

The following pseudocode illustrates the query-based inference interface of D4RT. Users provide a video clip and receive both a depth map and a set of tracked object predictions in a single forward pass: 

# D4RT Inference Interface (Pseudocode)

model = D4RT.from_pretrained("d4rt-base")

# Input: a clip of T consecutive frames (T=4 recommended)
frames = load_video_clip("scene_001.mp4", num_frames=4)
# frames.shape: (T, 3, H, W) with values in [0, 1]

# Single forward pass: reconstruction + tracking
output = model.infer(frames)

# Dense depth prediction
depth_map = output.depth              # shape: (H, W), meters
depth_conf = output.depth_confidence  # shape: (H, W), [0, 1]

# Multi-object tracking results
tracks = output.tracks                # list of Track objects
for track in tracks:
    print(f"  ID={track.id:03d}  "
          f"bbox={track.bbox.xyxy}  "
          f"score={track.score:.2f}")

# Export as point cloud with instance labels
pcd = output.to_point_cloud(min_confidence=0.7)
pcd.save("reconstruction.ply")

5. Training Objective

D4RT is trained end-to-end with a multi-task loss that balances depth reconstruction quality, tracking accuracy, and camera pose estimation. The total loss is a weighted sum of three component losses:

L = λ1 Ldepth + λ2 Ltrack + λ3 Lcamera

where Ldepth is the Scale-Invariant Logarithmic (SiLog) loss between predicted and ground-truth depth maps, Ltrack combines a focal loss for detection and a bipartite-matching loss for identity consistency, and Lcamera is a 6-DoF camera pose loss based on the geodesic distance between predicted and ground-truth poses. The weighting coefficients are set to λ1=1.0, λ2=0.5, and λ3=0.1 by default, and are annealed during training following a cosine schedule.

7. Discussion

The experimental results demonstrate that decoupling reconstruction and tracking through a query-based mechanism yields significant gains in both accuracy and efficiency. The shared query representation acts as an implicit scene prior: depth predictions benefit from knowing where objects are and how they move, while tracking benefits from understanding the 3D geometry that constrains plausible object motion. This mutual regularization effect is particularly evident in occlusion-heavy scenes, where D4RT's tracking accuracy drops by only 3.2% compared to D3DP's 9.7% degradation.

However, D4RT has limitations. The fixed query budget K places an upper bound on the number of simultaneously trackable objects; scenes with more than K independent entities will experience degraded tracking performance. Additionally, the method currently assumes a single dominant camera motion model and may struggle with highly non-rigid camera trajectories such as rapid rotations or significant shake. Extending D4RT to handle an unbounded number of objects and arbitrary camera motion remains an important direction for future work.
