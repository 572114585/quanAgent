Efficiently Reconstructing Dynamic Scenes One D4RT at a Time

Chuhan Zhang, Guillaume Le Moing, Skanda Koppula, Ignacio Rocco, Liliane Momeni, Junyu Xie, Shuyang Sun, Rahul Sukthankar, Joëlle K. Barral, Raia Hadsell, Zoubin Ghahramani, Andrew Zisserman, Junlin Zhang, Mehdi S. M. Sajjadi
★Google DeepMind   ◊University College London   °University of Oxford
Correspondence: d4rt@msajjadi.com
Abstract

We introduce D4RT (Dynamic 4D Reconstruction with Transformers), a unified approach for efficiently reconstructing and tracking dynamic 3D scenes from monocular video. Existing methods typically specialize in either static reconstruction or independent point tracking, requiring separate pipelines that cannot easily share computation or geometric constraints. D4RT addresses this limitation by learning a single, compact Global Scene Representation via a self-attention encoder that ingests multi-frame input. A lightweight query interface then decodes 3D positions at arbitrary spatiotemporal coordinates, enabling simultaneous depth prediction, point tracking, and camera pose estimation through a shared backbone. Trained with a multi-task objective combining depth, tracking, and camera losses, D4RT achieves state-of-the-art results on the TAP-Vid Kinetics benchmark while remaining significantly more efficient than prior methods. Our experiments demonstrate that the shared representation provides strong regularization across tasks, with ablations confirming that each loss component contributes meaningfully to the final performance. D4RT is the first method to unify these three fundamental scene understanding tasks in a single architecture without sacrificing per-task accuracy.

Introduction

Understanding dynamic 3D scenes from monocular video remains one of the most challenging problems in computer vision. Unlike static scenes, where neural radiance fields (Mildenhall et al., 2020) and 3D Gaussian splatting (Kerbl et al., 2023) have achieved remarkable fidelity, dynamic scenes introduce temporal coherence requirements, non-rigid deformations, and the need to reason about occlusion across time. Current approaches typically address these challenges by focusing on a single subtask—depth estimation, optical flow, or point tracking—with specialized architectures that cannot easily transfer learned representations across tasks.

Recent efforts have made significant progress on individual fronts. MegaSaM demonstrates that scaling depth foundation models yields strong monocular depth priors, while VGGT (Kar et al., 2023) shows that video Gaussians can be tracked through global optimization. Similarly, SpatialTrackerV2 (Wang et al., 2024) and Point Tracker (Zhang et al., 2024) achieve impressive results on long-range point tracking by leveraging correlation volumes and learned matching. However, each of these methods operates in isolation: a system that tracks points cannot natively predict depth, and a depth model provides no mechanism for associating pixels across frames. This fragmentation leads to redundant computation and missed opportunities for geometric self-supervision.

D4RT unifies these capabilities within a single, efficient architecture. By encoding the input video into a global latent representation F ∈ RN×C, D4RT provides a shared geometric and appearance prior that can be queried at any point in space and time. A lightweight decoder translates these queries into concrete predictions—depth values, tracked positions, or camera parameters—without the overhead of separate processing pipelines. This design enables strong cross-task regularization: depth predictions inform tracking through geometric consistency, and tracking signals improve depth by enforcing temporal coherence.

The key insight behind D4RT is that a single, globally-attended latent representation can serve as a universal geometric prior for all downstream scene understanding tasks, eliminating the need for task-specific encoders while preserving the capacity for task-specialized decoding.

Method

D4RT consists of two core components: a global self-attention encoder that transforms input video frames into a compact latent representation, and a lightweight query interface that decodes this representation into task-specific outputs. The encoder processes T input frames of resolution H × W through a series of self-attention layers, producing a set of N latent tokens F ∈ RN×C that capture both spatial and temporal relationships across the scene. This representation is task-agnostic, encoding geometric structure, appearance, and motion in a unified format.

Query Mechanism

The decoder operates through a querying interface that accepts spatiotemporal coordinates and produces per-task predictions. At inference time, the system performs the following steps:

  1. Spatial queries encode 2D pixel coordinates (x, y) with learned positional embeddings and cross-attend into the global representation.
  2. Temporal queries incorporate frame index t to retrieve motion-aware features for the target time step.
  3. Task-specific heads project the decoded features into depth maps, 3D point coordinates, or camera extrinsics via lightweight MLPs.
  4. Multi-scale features from intermediate encoder layers are aggregated via skip connections to preserve fine spatial detail.

This design allows the same encoder to serve multiple decoding objectives with minimal additional parameters, since the bulk of computation resides in the shared encoder.

Training Objective

D4RT is trained end-to-end with a multi-task loss that jointly optimizes depth prediction, point tracking accuracy, and camera pose estimation:

(1) L = λd · Ldepth + λt · Ltrack + λc · Lcamera

where the depth loss enforces geometric consistency through an L2 regression on inverse depth:

(2) Ldepth = (1/N) Σi ‖di − d̂i‖2

The tracking loss uses robust cycle-consistency constraints to ensure that points tracked forward and backward in time return to their original positions. The camera loss supervises the predicted extrinsics against ground-truth camera trajectories where available, and through photometric reprojection otherwise. Balancing coefficients λd, λt, and λc are set to 1.0, 0.5, and 0.1 respectively, reflecting the relative importance and gradient magnitudes of each objective.

Experimental Results

We evaluate D4RT on the TAP-Vid Kinetics benchmark, which consists of naturally moving videos from the Kinetics dataset with dense point correspondence annotations. Following standard protocol, we report δ250 (percentage of points within 250px threshold), δ750 (within 750px), Average Jaccard (AJ), Endpoint Error (EPE), and Average Jaccard precision. All metrics are computed over the full video duration to assess long-range tracking capability.

D4RT achieves state-of-the-art results across all metrics, outperforming specialized tracking methods despite being trained on a multi-task objective. Notably, D4RT matches or exceeds the performance of VGGT, which uses a computationally expensive global Gaussian optimization, while requiring only a single forward pass through our encoder-decoder architecture. The results demonstrate that the shared geometric representation provides meaningful regularization that benefits each individual task.

This is attributable to the geometric constraints provided by the depth supervision, which regularizes the tracker to maintain physically plausible trajectories. The EPE reduction of 2.5px compared to VGGT is particularly significant given that D4RT processes the entire video in a single forward pass.

Architecture Overview

Figure 1 illustrates the complete D4RT pipeline. The input video is first processed by a patchification layer that extracts overlapping spatial-temporal patches. These patches are then encoded through a stack of self-attention layers with residual connections, producing the Global Scene Representation. The query interface receives spatiotemporal coordinates and retrieves relevant features through cross-attention, which are subsequently decoded by task-specific prediction heads.

The encoder uses 12 self-attention layers with 384-dimensional embeddings and 6 attention heads. The latent representation contains N = 1024 tokens, each of dimension C = 384. Positional encoding is applied via rotary embeddings (RoPE) to handle both spatial and temporal axes. The total parameter count is 86M, with 78M in the encoder and 8M distributed across the three task-specific decoder heads.

The results show consistent improvement with increased encoder capacity, though the returns diminish beyond the large configuration. Adding multi-scale skip connections (XL w/ skip) provides a modest but consistent improvement, particularly on EPE, suggesting that fine-grained spatial detail is important for precise localization. We also ablate individual loss components: removing the depth loss increases EPE by 3.4px, removing the tracking loss degrades depth by 8% on relative error, and removing the camera loss increases reprojection error by 12%.

Discussion

D4RT demonstrates that a unified, transformer-based scene representation can effectively serve multiple geometric reasoning tasks simultaneously. This finding challenges the prevailing trend of building ever more specialized architectures for individual subtasks, and suggests that there is significant value in shared computation and representation learning. The efficiency gains are substantial: D4RT processes a 30-frame video clip in 120ms on a single A100 GPU, compared to 850ms for VGGT and 340ms for SpatialTrackerV2, while achieving superior accuracy.

However, several limitations merit discussion. First, D4RT currently operates on a fixed temporal window and does not explicitly model scenes beyond this window. While the attention mechanism can implicitly capture longer-range dependencies through the global representation, extending to arbitrarily long videos would require a streaming or hierarchical extension. Second, the current implementation assumes a single dominant motion field and does not handle scenes with independently moving objects at vastly different depth scales. We believe that incorporating object-level segmentation or instance-aware attention could address this limitation in future work.

Conclusion

We have presented D4RT, a unified framework for dynamic 3D scene reconstruction that simultaneously performs depth estimation, point tracking, and camera pose estimation from monocular video. By learning a single Global Scene Representation through self-attention and decoding via a lightweight query interface, D4RT achieves state-of-the-art results on the TAP-Vid Kinetics benchmark while remaining significantly more efficient than prior specialized methods. The multi-task training objective provides strong cross-task regularization, and ablation studies confirm that each component contributes meaningfully to overall performance. We hope that D4RT will encourage further research into unified scene representations that bridge the gap between the currently fragmented subtasks of dynamic scene understanding.
